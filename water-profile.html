The sandstone aquifer

PEI sits on a fractured red sandstone formation that functions as the island's sole source of potable water. Unlike deep, confined aquifers in other provinces, PEI's aquifer is shallow and unconfined — meaning surface conditions directly affect water quality within weeks to months, not years.

Rain and snowmelt percolate through the soil and rock to recharge the aquifer continuously. This makes PEI highly sensitive to both drought (insufficient recharge) and agricultural contamination (rapid infiltration of surface chemicals). The same geology that makes the island productive farmland makes it vulnerable.

Approximately 50% of Islanders are served by central water systems in Charlottetown, Summerside, Stratford, Cornwall, and several smaller municipalities. The other half draw from private wells — and those residents bear the most direct exposure to aquifer quality changes.

The nitrate problem

Nitrogen fertilizers used in potato production — PEI grows roughly one-third of Canada's potatoes — convert to nitrate-nitrogen in soil and migrate downward into groundwater. This is the island's most persistent water quality challenge.

The provincial mean nitrate concentration across monitored watersheds is approximately 3.3 mg/L N-NO₃, well below the 10 mg/L Health Canada drinking water guideline. However, agricultural watersheds in Queens County — particularly the Dunk River and Westmorland River catchments — regularly exceed 5 mg/L, and individual wells have recorded values above 20 mg/L.

Elevated nitrate poses the greatest risk to infants under six months, where it interferes with blood oxygen transport (methemoglobinemia, or "blue baby syndrome"). For adults, chronic exposure above guideline levels has been associated with increased colorectal cancer risk in recent epidemiological literature.

Key risks

High — Agricultural contamination
Nitrate migration from potato production is chronic and structural. It will not resolve without sustained changes to fertilizer application rates and riparian buffer enforcement.
High — Drought stress on recharge
Summers with below-normal precipitation directly lower the water table. Combined with increased irrigation demand, drought years can produce aquifer stress within a single season.
Medium — Coastal saltwater intrusion
In coastal areas, excessive groundwater withdrawal or sea level rise can allow saltwater to migrate into freshwater wells — a growing concern as the island's coastline erodes at 0.3–1.0m/year in some areas.
Medium — Central system aging
The Charlottetown water treatment and distribution system has significant aging infrastructure. Main breaks trigger boil water advisories and can expose tens of thousands of users to risk.
Lower — Bacterial events (post-storm)
Intense rainfall can cause surface runoff to overwhelm treatment capacity or infiltrate distribution systems. These events are typically short-lived but require rapid response.

Why the aquifer z-score matters

The 17 observation wells in the OD0038 dataset measure depth-to-water in metres — how far below the surface the water table sits. A higher reading means a deeper (lower) water table; a lower reading means a shallower (higher) water table. This creates a counterintuitive number for general audiences.

The dashboard converts these raw depths to a z-score: the departure from each well's historical average for that calendar month, expressed in standard deviations. A z-score of +1.5 means the water table is 1.5 standard deviations below normal for the season — a meaningful drought signal. A z-score of -1.0 means levels are above normal — good recharge.

Averaging across all active wells gives a provincial picture. This approach normalizes across wells with wildly different depths — Georgetown sits at ~4m, Caledonia at ~83m — and focuses on the departure from normal rather than the absolute level, which varies naturally by geology and location.

Data lag: OD0038 is updated monthly, typically with a 2–4 month lag. The indicator reflects recent conditions, not real-time status.
